# Literature Review

## Physician and Advanced Practice Provider Compensation: Historical Context, Market Evolution, and Future Trajectories

### Introduction

The economic landscape of healthcare delivery in the United States has undergone profound transformation over the past several decades, fundamentally reshaping how physicians and advanced practice providers (APPs) are compensated, organized, and deployed within health systems. The evolution of compensation models reflects broader shifts in healthcare policy, market consolidation, workforce dynamics, and the transition from volume-based to value-based care. This review synthesizes current research on compensation trends, workforce supply and demand, and the structural forces reshaping the healthcare labor market as of 2026.

---

### Historical Context of Physician Compensation

Historically, physician compensation in the United States was dominated by fee-for-service models that rewarded volume of services delivered. Under this structure, compensation was directly correlated with the number of procedures performed, office visits conducted, and ancillary services ordered (Medicare and Medicaid Services, 2024). The fee-for-service paradigm dominated throughout the latter half of the 20th century, incentivizing increased service utilization without necessarily correlating compensation to quality or outcomes.

The Affordable Care Act (ACA) of 2010 initiated a systematic shift toward value-based reimbursement models, creating new pressures on compensation structures that persist to the present day. According to Doximity's 2024 Physician Compensation Report, national average compensation has experienced modest year-over-year growth, though with significant variation across specialties and geographic regions, reflecting the complex interplay between traditional volume-based income models and emerging quality-based incentives. Cost-of-living adjustments have become increasingly critical factors in determining real earnings, with certain metropolitan areas—such as Las Vegas, New Orleans, and Jacksonville—consistently ranking among the top 10 highest-compensated regions across both 2023 and 2024 reporting cycles (Doximity, 2024).

The Medical Group Management Association (MGMA) 2024 Provider Compensation Report, drawing from data on over 211,000 physicians and APPs, further documents this transition, demonstrating that specialty-specific compensation ranges and productivity metrics (measured in work Relative Value Units, or wRVUs) have become central features of contemporary compensation structures (MGMA, 2024). These metrics represent the ongoing tension between volume-driven and quality-driven compensation philosophies.

---

### Evolution of APP Roles in Healthcare Employment

Advanced Practice Providers—encompassing nurse practitioners (NPs) and physician assistants (PAs)—have experienced explosive growth in both workforce size and scope of practice over the past two decades. Originally conceived as physician extenders in the mid-20th century to address workforce shortages, APPs now serve as primary care providers, specialists, surgical assistants, and critical components of team-based care delivery models (Marit Health, 2024).

Compensation for APPs has accelerated significantly in recent years. Marit Health's 2024–2025 Contract Trends Report documents 14% year-over-year salary growth for NPs, with average earnings rising from $121,000 in 2022 to $135,000 in 2023 (Marit Health, 2024–2025). Sullivan Cotter's 2025 APP Compensation Survey corroborates these findings, reporting significant increases in total cash compensation across all APP specialties, with the highest growth rates observed in surgical and procedural specialties—up 6–7% year-over-year (Sullivan Cotter, 2025). The expanding role of APPs in primary care and chronic disease management has fundamentally influenced how organizations structure their compensation packages, increasing the use of flexible work arrangements, incentive structures, and performance-based bonuses (Sullivan Cotter, 2025; Marit Health, 2024–2025).

ECG Management Consultants' 2025 Physician and APP Compensation Survey, encompassing 27 APP specialties, reveals that regional variations increasingly complicate compensation benchmarking and that diverse compensation structures—including salary plus bonus, profit-sharing arrangements, and emerging equity-based models—now characterize the APP labor market (ECG, 2025). The Marit Health report further notes increasing demand for flexible work arrangements and incentive structures, suggesting that compensation has become multidimensional beyond base salary alone.

Notably, Sullivan Cotter has also documented growing pay compression within APP workforces, as organizations increasingly address patient access challenges by deploying APPs in higher-acuity roles previously reserved for physicians (Sullivan Cotter, 2025). This pay compression phenomenon raises important questions about role differentiation, scope of practice, and long-term workforce sustainability.

---

### Healthcare Consolidation and Private Equity

The consolidation of physician practices into larger health systems and the increasing involvement of private equity in healthcare delivery represent two of the most consequential forces reshaping compensation structures. The U.S. Government Accountability Office (GAO, 2025) reports that physician consolidation has conferred increased market power to large health organizations, fundamentally altering the compensation dynamics for both physicians and APPs. Hospital-employed physicians now frequently operate under different compensation models compared to their private practice counterparts, with employed providers more likely to receive guaranteed base salaries, quality-based incentives, and benefits packages that differ significantly from production-oriented independent practice arrangements (GAO, 2025).

The growth of hospital-employed positions has shifted the physician compensation landscape. Historically, the majority of U.S. physicians operated in independent or small group practices, where compensation was directly tied to practice revenue. As health systems acquire physician practices at accelerating rates, compensation has shifted toward salaried models with productivity thresholds and quality-based bonus structures. Sullivan Cotter reports that physician total cash compensation is experiencing the highest increases of the current decade, driven in part by this consolidation dynamic and the resulting competitive pressure for talent among increasingly large organizational entities (Sullivan Cotter, 2025, BusinessWire).

Private equity investment in physician group acquisitions has similarly reshaped compensation expectations and workforce dynamics. While specific data on private equity-driven compensation changes remains limited in the academic literature, industry analyses suggest that PE-backed practices tend to emphasize productivity-based compensation and cost efficiency, potentially creating different incentive environments compared to nonprofit or publicly owned health systems. The interplay between consolidation, PE involvement, and compensation model design represents an active area requiring further empirical investigation.

---

### The Impact of Value-Based Care on Compensation

The transition from fee-for-service to value-based payment models has been perhaps the most philosophically significant development in healthcare compensation policy. Value-based care programs—including Medicare's Merit-Based Incentive Payment System (MIPS), Accountable Care Organizations (ACOs), and bundled payment initiatives—have introduced performance metrics, quality benchmarks, and cost-efficiency targets as central features of reimbursement and, by extension, physician and APP compensation.

Becker's Physician Leadership (2024) identifies the shifting economics of physician pay as characterized by several key trends: increasing emphasis on quality metrics tied to compensation, transition from pure volume-based wRVU calculations to blended productivity and quality models, and growing organizational attention to productivity-based adjustments rather than pure volume metrics alone (Becker's Physician Leadership, 2024). These trends reflect the broader healthcare system's attempt to align financial incentives with patient outcomes rather than service volume.

Market analyses from ECG (2025) and Sullivan Cotter (2025) demonstrate that organizations are increasingly incorporating quality measures, patient satisfaction scores, and population health outcomes into compensation formulas, although the extent and sophistication of these incorporation varies widely across specialties and organizational types. The tension between maintaining adequate physician income in the face of potentially lower reimbursement per service (under value-based models) and the organizational imperative to control costs continues to shape the design and evolution of compensation arrangements.

---

### Workforce Supply and Demand Dynamics

The supply and demand imbalance in physician and APP workforces represents a critical structural force driving compensation growth and organizational strategy. The American Association of Medical Colleges (AAMC) projects physician shortages ranging from 46,900 to 90,000 by 2034, with particularly acute gaps anticipated in primary care, rural medicine, and certain specialty areas (AAMC, 2024). This projected shortage creates competitive labor markets that exert upward pressure on compensation, particularly in specialties and geographic regions where supply is most constrained.

The Doximity 2024 report notes that compensation is increasingly deployed as a retention tool, particularly for specialties with high burnout rates, further intensifying competition in an already tight labor market (Doximity, 2024). Burnout and workforce sustainability have emerged as critical considerations in compensation design, with organizations investing in enhanced financial incentives, reduced clinical loads, and improved work-life balance offerings to retain and attract physicians in high-stress specialties (Doximity, 2024).

The APP workforce continues to expand as a partial response to physician supply constraints. Sullivan Cotter's 2025 data documents strong demand growth across all APP specialties, while Marit Health's analysis of NP compensation trajectories confirms the sustained upward pressure on APP salaries as organizations increasingly rely on these practitioners to fill gaps in care delivery (Sullivan Cotter, 2025; Marit Health, 2024–2025). The convergence of physician shortages, APP workforce expansion, and escalating compensation across both groups suggests that healthcare organizations face sustained cost pressures related to workforce investment for the foreseeable future.

---

### Synthesis and Research Gaps

The literature reviewed here reveals a compensation landscape in rapid transition. Several themes emerge consistently across multiple industry reports and data sources:

1. **Compensation growth is accelerating** after a period of relative stagnation, with Sullivan Cotter reporting the highest cash compensation increases of the current decade for physicians and double-digit salary growth for APPs.

2. **Compensation models are diversifying** beyond pure volume-based metrics to incorporate quality measures, patient outcomes, retention incentives, and flexible work arrangements.

3. **Structural market forces**—consolidation, private equity involvement, value-based care mandates, and workforce shortages—are simultaneously driving both the level and the composition of provider compensation.

4. **The APP compensation gap is narrowing** as these practitioners assume increasingly complex roles, raising important questions about the future of role differentiation and team-based care economics.

Despite the breadth of industry survey data available, significant research gaps remain. Much of the current evidence base relies on proprietary industry surveys rather than peer-reviewed empirical research. Long-term studies tracking the effects of value-based compensation models on patient outcomes, physician well-being, and health system financial sustainability are limited. Furthermore, the impact of private equity ownership on compensation structures and care delivery economics remains understudied in the academic literature.
